## What Is E&S Insurance, and Why Does It Matter?

Before understanding Kinsale, you need to understand its market: the **Excess & Surplus (E&S) lines** segment of the U.S. insurance industry.

Standard (admitted) insurance carriers must file their policy forms and rates with each state's department of insurance and get approval before using them. That works fine for routine risks — a typical restaurant, a small office building, a standard auto fleet. But it completely breaks down for unusual, complex, or high-hazard risks: a marijuana dispensary, a construction project with a complicated liability tower, a tech startup with novel cyber exposure, or a homeowner in a wildfire zone. Standard carriers either won't touch these risks, or can't price them competitively because the regulatory approval process is too slow.

The E&S market solves this. E&S carriers are "non-admitted" — exempt from rate and form filing requirements — so they can write policies on their own terms, price risks individually, and move fast. The trade-off for policyholders is that E&S policies don't carry state guaranty fund backing if the insurer fails, which is why E&S carriers must demonstrate financial strength. Kinsale holds an **A (Excellent) AM Best rating**.

Risks flow into E&S through **surplus lines brokers** — specialized intermediaries who must first document that they tried to place a risk in the admitted market and failed. The U.S. E&S market surpassed **$100 billion in direct written premiums** for the first time in 2025, growing at roughly 13–14% annually in recent years.

## The Business Model

Kinsale is the **only publicly traded pure-play E&S insurer** in the United States. Founded in 2009 by CEO Michael Kehoe, it went public in July 2016 at $16/share.

**How it makes money:**

1. **Underwriting profit** — Kinsale collects premiums, pays reinsurers for catastrophe protection, and retains net earned premiums. After paying claims and operating expenses, the remainder is underwriting income. In 2025, it generated $2.0 billion in gross written premiums and converted that into a **75.9% combined ratio** — meaning it spent only 75.9 cents on losses and expenses for every dollar of premium collected.

2. **Investment income** — Insurance companies hold "float" — the time gap between collecting premiums and paying claims. Kinsale invests this (~$4.5 billion investment portfolio, ~87% fixed income) for additional income. In Q1 2026, net investment income was **$55.4 million, up 26.5% year over year**.

**What it writes:** Commercial property, general liability, commercial auto, professional liability, excess casualty, product liability, inland marine, agribusiness, small business property, and personal surplus lines. Deliberately targets **small-to-medium accounts** (average policy premium ~$12,200 in Q1 2026) where competition is less intense and margins are higher.

## What Makes Kinsale Unique

### 1. Proprietary Technology Built from Scratch

Most insurance companies run on off-the-shelf systems like Guidewire or Duck Creek — legacy platforms that are slow to customize and expensive to automate. Kinsale spent seven years building its own **end-to-end proprietary platform** covering quoting, policy issuance, billing, claims management, and data analytics.

The concrete effects:
- An underwriter at Kinsale can process roughly **10 submissions per day** vs. 2–3 at a typical competitor — a 3–5x productivity advantage
- Straight-through processing for standard risks reduces manual touchpoints
- Proprietary loss data feeds pricing models, improving underwriting accuracy over time — a **compounding data moat**
- No legacy technical debt means faster adoption of new tools, including AI (deployed in 2025 for underwriting and claims automation)

### 2. Pure-Play Focus Creates Total Organizational Alignment

Every large E&S competitor — W.R. Berkley, Markel, Arch Capital, Hartford — has an E&S division, but none are pure-play. For competitors, E&S is a department. For Kinsale, it is the **entire enterprise**. Every hire, every system, every incentive is oriented around E&S profitability. This focus is itself a competitive advantage that cannot be replicated by a conglomerate.

### 3. The Low-Cost Provider Model

Kinsale explicitly positions itself as the **low-cost provider** in the E&S market. Its expense ratio (~21%) is roughly **half the industry average** (30–40% for most P&C carriers). This is analogous to GEICO's model in personal auto but applied to specialty commercial lines.

The implication: even at more competitive pricing, Kinsale remains profitable where rivals cannot be. At a 30% expense ratio and 60% loss ratio, a competitor breaks even. At those same economics, Kinsale earns a **20% underwriting profit margin**.

### 4. In-House Underwriting and Claims, End to End

Unlike MGAs (managing general agents) who write policies on behalf of a carrier without bearing the risk, Kinsale's underwriters are employees making decisions for the carrier's own balance sheet. This eliminates principal-agent conflicts. Claims are also handled entirely in-house. Management believes control over both ends of the value chain is essential to underwriting discipline — and the combined ratio proves it.

### 5. Founder-Led with Strong Alignment

Michael Kehoe, the founder, remains Chairman and CEO. He owns approximately **3.82% of the company** (~$430 million at recent prices), creating strong alignment with shareholders. The company's culture emphasizes an owner mindset throughout the workforce via equity participation.

## Growth Prospects: The Structural Tailwinds

Kinsale wrote ~$2.0 billion in gross premiums in 2025 against a **$100+ billion E&S market** — roughly 1.6–2% market share. The runway is long.

### Why the E&S market keeps growing

1. **Climate risk**: Wildfires, flooding, hurricanes, and extreme weather are causing admitted carriers to exit large parts of the U.S. (notably California, Florida, and the Gulf Coast). Risks that lose admitted coverage flow directly into E&S. The E&S homeowners segment has grown at a **34% CAGR over three years**.

2. **Social inflation**: Runaway jury awards and third-party litigation funding have made casualty liability increasingly unpredictable. Standard carriers restrict capacity; E&S carriers who can price more flexibly pick up the flow.

3. **Novel risks**: Autonomous vehicles, cannabis, cryptocurrency, AI liability, gig-economy workers — every new class of hard-to-place risk defaults to E&S.

4. **Casualty growth**: The E&S casualty marketplace is seeing significant growth in submissions driven by standard carriers pulling back from complex risks.

### Near-Term Headwind — Know What You're Buying

The **commercial property segment** (particularly large layered accounts) has faced significant pricing pressure as overcapacity flowed in from the standard market and Bermuda. Kinsale's commercial property gross written premiums fell **17.9% in full-year 2025** and **28.3% in Q4 2025**. This is the primary driver of the headline revenue growth slowdown.

Critically, excluding commercial property, Kinsale grew **13.3% in 2025**. Management is deliberately rotating away from large-property accounts toward casualty, personal lines, and small commercial — where margins remain robust. This is disciplined underwriting, not a business problem.

A high P/B ratio (~5x) sounds rich until you consider that **a company compounding book value at 28–32% annually deserves a premium to book**. The math: at a 30% ROE, book value doubles roughly every 2.5 years. Paying 5x book for a 30% ROE compounder is standard for quality businesses.

The current ~16x trailing P/E is the most attractive entry point in years — possibly since 2017. Historically, buying KNSL when the trailing P/E compressed into the low-to-mid 20s has delivered strong returns over a 2–3 year horizon. At 16x, the setup is better.

**The key risk**: if E&S market pricing softens broadly beyond commercial property — extending to casualty and small commercial — combined ratios would rise and 2026 earnings estimates would face downside pressure. The current price is pricing in margin compression in property but a resilient casualty and small-commercial book. Q1 2026 operating EPS of $5.11 (+37.7% YoY) and a 75.2% combined ratio suggest the thesis is intact, but this is the main variable to watch.
