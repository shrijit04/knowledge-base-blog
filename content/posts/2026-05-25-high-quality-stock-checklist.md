Most investors spend years chasing tips before they stop and ask the more useful question: *what does a genuinely high-quality business actually look like?*

This checklist is an attempt to answer that. Each point is a filter. No business will pass every single one, but the more boxes checked, the more durable the investment thesis. Each sub-point below will get its own deep-dive — this is the map, not the territory.

---

## 1. Business Quality & Moat

**Economies of scale** — As the business grows, its cost per unit falls and its margins and FCF expand. Scale itself deepens the moat. Asian Paints spent decades building a distribution network so wide that competitors can't replicate it at a comparable cost; that scale now makes every new litre of paint more profitable than the last.

**Strong moat — and one that strengthens over time.** The best moats compound:

| Moat type | Indian example | Global example |
|---|---|---|
| Brand | Titan, Nestlé India | Apple |
| Switching costs | Tally, CAMS | SAP, Oracle |
| Network effects | CDSL, NSE | Visa, Mastercard |
| IP / Patents | Divi's Labs | TSMC |
| Data | Info Edge (Naukri) | Google |
| Cost advantage | D-Mart (operational) | Costco |

**Pricing power** — The business can pass on inflation without losing market share. This only works with a real moat — in commodities and auto OEMs, pricing wars erode whatever advantage briefly appeared. Royal Enfield, Colgate, and TSMC can raise prices precisely because their customers have no comparable alternative.

**Low capital intensity** — Low capex requirements improve FCF, raise ROCE, and free up capital for faster expansion. TCS and Infosys grow revenue while spending a fraction of earnings on fixed assets. D-Mart runs ~3× asset turns on its base. Contrast with steel or telecom, where every rupee of growth requires another rupee of capex.

**Longevity and innovation** — The business model must be able to survive for decades. That requires embracing technology rather than ignoring it. A company not investing in R&D to protect its moat is slowly eating its own future.

---

## 2. Financial Strength

**High ROCE (target: consistently above 20–25%)** — ROCE is the single best measure of capital efficiency. Page Industries has sustained ROCE above 40% for over a decade. A declining ROCE over multiple years is one of the earliest signals that a moat is eroding.

**High, stable, growing FCF** — The real test is not earnings but cash. A business that needs more and more capital to produce the same cash is losing its edge. Scrutinise: *Is FCF growing faster than capex?* TCS and Nestlé India generate substantial free cash year after year with minimal reinvestment requirements.

**High margins** — Gross margin reflects the strength of the *business*. Operating margin reflects the strength of *management*. A wide, stable gross margin (like Divi's Labs at 65–67% for 10 years) signals structural advantage. Watch the gross-to-operating gap — companies burning cash on admin and marketing without matching revenue growth are waving a flag.

**Strong balance sheet — avoid leverage.** Debt amplifies both gains and losses. In a downturn, a leveraged business faces a double squeeze: falling revenue *and* fixed interest obligations. Debt-free or low-debt businesses — TCS, Infosys, Asian Paints, Titan — have the flexibility to invest counter-cyclically when weaker competitors are scrambling to survive. As the saying goes: *leverage, ladies, and liquor can destroy any business or any person.*

---

## 3. Management & Capital Allocation

**Founder-driven leadership.** Founders tend to think in decades, not quarters. They are bolder risk-takers, better capital allocators, and have stronger conviction in their own vision. ICICI Securities research found that Indian 100-baggers shared two traits: a focused business model and management with unwavering strategic clarity — almost always owner-operators.

Examples from the user's own research: Divi's Labs, Apollo Hospitals, HDFC Bank, Titan, Asian Paints, Bajaj Finance, Havells, Eicher Motors. Globally: Meta, Airbnb. *The 100-baggers are almost always founder-driven.*

**Promoter skin in the game.** Increasing promoter holding is a strong positive signal. A 2–3% reduction over time is acceptable. A 20–30% reduction is a red flag — it means the person who knows the business best is choosing to exit. In the current Indian market, this is exactly what's happening in several low-quality mid-caps. Check the trend, not just the number.

**Reinvestment opportunities — organic, not credit-driven.** Look for long structural tailwinds that don't depend on a credit cycle. Cybersecurity (global) and formalisation of previously unorganised sectors (Titan over the kirana jeweller, Vedant/Manyavar over unbranded ethnic wear) are strong examples. *Caveat*: both Titan and Vedant currently trade at valuations that make entry difficult — they tick most boxes but not the valuation one.

**Acquisition discipline.** Growth through M&A is fine if it strengthens the core business, was funded by FCF (not debt), and management has a track record of integration success. Red flags: acquiring unrelated businesses to chase trends, using debt for acquisitions, and neglecting the original core business in the process. Study the last 3–5 acquisitions before forming a view.

## 4. Consistency & Track Record

**Consistent EPS growth.** Pull a 10-year EPS chart on Screener. A high-quality business shows a smooth upward curve. A cyclical business shows peaks and troughs. Avoid the cyclicals unless you're specifically playing a cycle — they are easily confused with compounders during bull markets.

**Read 5–10 years of annual reports** — or at minimum, the Chairman's commentary. Ask: did management deliver what they said they would deliver? A great track record of under-promise and over-deliver is worth more than any forward guidance. Most companies talk. Few show it in their financials.

---

## 5. Valuation

**Reasonable PE — never overpay.** A stock at 80–100× earnings has already priced in years of future growth. For that to be justified, the company needs to sustain 50–60%+ EPS CAGR for multiple years — which almost never happens. Buying at that entry point usually results in wealth destruction even when the business continues to do well. The PE engine (see [Two Engine Framework](/posts/frameworks/two-engine-framework/)) works *for* you at entry, not against you.

**Invest in crisis.** Durable businesses go on sale during broad market panics, sector corrections, or a few quarters of slow EPS growth — without any permanent damage to the business model. March 2020 offered HDFC Bank at ~₹740 (vs. ₹1,200 before and higher since), Asian Paints at prices not seen in years. The checklist doesn't change in a downturn — if the business still passes, the price just got better.

---

## 6. Investor Behaviour

**Avoid poor-quality and commodity businesses — even when they're rising.** A stock going up is not evidence of a good business. Commodity stocks and low-moat businesses can run 2–3× on a tailwind and give it all back when the cycle turns. The checklist will filter most of these out if applied honestly.

**Don't over-diversify.** Academic research (Evans & Archer, 1968) shows that a 20-stock portfolio eliminates roughly 90% of diversifiable risk. Beyond 25 stocks, the marginal risk reduction from adding another position approaches zero — but your attention, conviction, and return potential all decline. Invest in your top 20–25 ideas. Your 100th-best idea has no place in the portfolio.

**Understand the business before you buy.** If you can't explain in plain language what the company does and why customers pay it — don't invest. During a drawdown you will need the conviction to hold. That conviction comes from understanding, not from a price chart.

**Avoid frequent trading.** Every trade is a tax event, a transaction cost, and a compounding interruption. The math of long-term compounding is ruthless — frequent in-and-out destroys exactly the returns that patience was building.

**Be patient and disciplined.** This is not filler advice. It's the variable that separates the investor who executes this checklist and builds wealth from the one who executes it perfectly but sells at the first correction.
