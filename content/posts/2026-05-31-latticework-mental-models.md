Charlie Munger called it a *latticework* — a web of mental models across disciplines that, together, give you a far sharper picture of reality than any single lens. The models below span business, behaviour, learning, and life. Each one is simple. The power is in stacking them.

---

{{< principle
  name="Take Simple Ideas Seriously"
  tagline="The edge is in execution, not complexity."
  explanation="Simple ideas consistently applied outperform complex ones inconsistently executed. Munger built his worldview on a handful of reliable mental models applied relentlessly across every domain. The failure mode isn't missing obscure knowledge — it's dismissing obvious truths as too basic to bother with."
  relevance="Strategy, learning, building habits, any domain where fundamentals are undervalued in favour of sophistication."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Truth and Trust Compound"
  tagline="Small lies are the rust that takes down the whole structure."
  explanation="Trust accumulates slowly and evaporates fast. A reputation for honesty compounds over years into a durable advantage — in business, relationships, and leadership. Small lies feel cheap in the moment but carry compounding interest: they require more lies to sustain, erode self-trust, and leave you managing fiction instead of reality."
  relevance="Every relationship and institution. Especially high-stakes partnerships where the cost of a breach is asymmetric."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Build Extreme Trust With All Stakeholders"
  tagline="Trust is the cheapest form of leverage."
  explanation="Customers, employees, investors, and partners who trust you transact faster, require less oversight, and stay longer. Trust reduces friction at every level of an organisation. It cannot be bought — only earned, then protected. Companies that treat trust as an asset to be invested in rather than a constraint to be managed consistently outperform."
  relevance="Business building, leadership, partnerships, any long-term relationship where the compounding of goodwill matters more than short-term extraction."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Deep Desire Shapes Destiny"
  tagline="What you truly want — not what you say you want — determines your direction."
  explanation="Stated goals are easy. Revealed preferences — where you actually spend your time, energy, and attention — are the real map of your future. Motivation follows genuine desire, not willpower. The most important career and life decision is figuring out what you deeply want, before optimising anything else."
  relevance="Career choices, goal-setting, habit formation, understanding why plans repeatedly fail despite apparent effort."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Belief Comes Before Capability"
  tagline="You cannot build what you cannot first imagine as possible."
  explanation="Capability develops from sustained effort. Sustained effort requires belief that the outcome is achievable. Doubt is a self-fulfilling ceiling — it caps effort before it caps results. Every skill you now have was once something you believed you could learn. The same logic applies to everything you haven't yet tried."
  relevance="Learning new skills, starting new ventures, overcoming fear of failure, coaching and managing others."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Seek Asymmetric Bets"
  tagline="Big upside, capped downside. Everything else is optional."
  explanation="Most great outcomes come from a small number of outsized wins, not a long series of modest ones. Actively seek situations where the cost of being wrong is small and the reward for being right is large — in investments, career moves, experiments, and relationships. Avoid the reverse: situations where you can only lose big or win small."
  relevance="Investing, career decisions, business strategy, product experiments, any domain where optionality and downside protection matter."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="You Don't Need Much Capital to Start"
  tagline="Start small, start real, start now."
  explanation="The best businesses validate early with minimal investment. Capital-heavy starts are usually a sign of unvalidated assumptions, not ambition. Most founding mistakes happen before the first customer; more money just funds those mistakes at scale. A small, honest experiment tells you more than any business plan."
  relevance="Entrepreneurship, side projects, testing new ideas, any situation where waiting for 'enough resources' is the actual obstacle."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Don't Quit Your Job Too Early"
  tagline="Runway is a feature, not a compromise."
  explanation="Premature resignation is one of the most common avoidable causes of startup failure. Financial pressure compresses time horizons and forces bad decisions — accepting wrong customers, under-pricing, hiring too fast. Stay until the venture can pay you, or until not staying is clearly the larger risk. Patience at this stage is not timidity; it's capital efficiency."
  relevance="Starting a business, going independent, any transition where financial security directly affects the quality of decisions."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Shamelessly Clone What Works"
  tagline="Originality is overrated. Execution is everything."
  explanation="Study what the best in your field do, then copy it relentlessly until you understand it well enough to improve it. Picasso: 'Good artists copy, great artists steal.' Most businesses fail trying to be original when the market already has a validated template. Clone the model, then add your own edge."
  relevance="Product development, business building, skill acquisition, marketing strategy, any domain where proven patterns exist."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Spend Time With People Better Than You"
  tagline="Your environment sets the standard you unconsciously calibrate to."
  explanation="Willpower is overrated; environment is underrated. The people around you define what feels normal — for ambition, for standards, for behaviour. Deliberately spending time with people who are ahead of you raises your internal benchmark. It's one of the highest-leverage investments you can make in yourself."
  relevance="Career growth, personal development, finding mentors, choosing co-founders and early employees."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Hire for Integrity, Intelligence, Energy, and Capability"
  tagline="One missing corner collapses the whole structure."
  explanation="Buffett's framework: integrity without intelligence is well-meaning but ineffective; intelligence without integrity is actively dangerous; energy without both is chaos. Capability — the ability to actually execute — is the fourth leg. A team with all four is rare. Finding it is the most valuable thing a leader can spend time on."
  relevance="Hiring, team building, choosing co-founders, evaluating management quality in companies you invest in."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Incentives Dominate Behaviour"
  tagline="Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."
  explanation="Munger called this his most useful mental model. Almost every organisational failure, corrupt institution, or baffling human behaviour makes immediate sense once you map the incentives. Before blaming people, check the system. Before designing a system, check what behaviour it inadvertently rewards."
  relevance="Management, policy design, understanding corporate behaviour, evaluating company cultures, diagnosing why good people do bad things."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Pursue Extreme Quality"
  tagline="Average quality is a race to the bottom. Extreme quality compounds."
  explanation="Quality is a compounding asset. It builds reputation, generates word-of-mouth, creates pricing power, and attracts better people — all without marketing spend. Average quality requires constant defensive effort to maintain position. Extreme quality creates a moat. The cost of going from good to great is smaller than most people assume; the return is far larger."
  relevance="Product development, service delivery, creative work, brand building, any context where reputation is the primary competitive asset."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Serve First; Money Follows"
  tagline="Build genuine value before extracting it."
  explanation="This is not just ethics — it is strategy. Businesses that lead with service build trust and loyalty that sustains them when competition arrives. Extractive approaches optimise for the first transaction at the expense of all subsequent ones. In the long run, the most profitable businesses are the ones customers genuinely wanted to exist."
  relevance="Business building, sales, leadership, any relationship where trust determines the size of the long-term opportunity."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Protect and Value Your Time"
  tagline="Every yes is a no to something else."
  explanation="Time is the only truly non-renewable resource. Unlike money, it cannot be earned back. Every commitment is a trade — what you say yes to is bought with time that could have gone somewhere else. The highest-leverage question is not 'can I do this?' but 'is this the best use of the time it requires?'"
  relevance="Prioritisation, managing commitments, building focus, evaluating opportunities, any situation where saying no is the underused superpower."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Stack Models; Compounding Is the Real Power"
  tagline="One model is a lens. A latticework is a worldview."
  explanation="Each mental model you truly internalise doesn't add to your thinking — it multiplies, because it interacts with every model you already have. A person with ten deeply understood models across disciplines will consistently outthink a specialist who knows one domain at depth. This is the core argument for the latticework approach."
  relevance="Learning strategy, decision-making, building judgment over time, understanding why generalists often outperform specialists on complex problems."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Customers Reveal the Real Business"
  tagline="What people pay for is the business. What you think they pay for is a theory."
  explanation="Customers vote with money and behaviour, not with surveys. What they actually buy — and what they tell their friends to buy — defines the real product and the real value proposition, which is often not what the founder intended. The fastest way to understand a business is to listen to its best customers."
  relevance="Product development, go-to-market strategy, pivoting, diagnosing stagnation, evaluating companies as an investor."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Own a Segment; Don't Chase a Slice of a Huge Market"
  tagline="1% of a $10B market is not a strategy. It's a wish."
  explanation="Dominating a small, well-defined segment creates real defensibility. Spreading thin across a huge market creates none. The companies that eventually capture large markets almost always started by owning a narrow niche completely — Amazon with books, Google with search, Titan with the organised jewellery segment in India. Own first. Expand second."
  relevance="Business strategy, product positioning, market entry, go-to-market planning."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Use Superior Filters for Learning"
  tagline="Input quality determines output quality."
  explanation="In an era of infinite information, the constraint is not access — it is filtration. Returns on learning are driven by signal quality, not volume. A single well-chosen book outperforms a hundred mediocre blog posts. Build deliberate systems for finding and prioritising the best sources: trusted curators, primary texts, people who have done the thing."
  relevance="Learning strategy, content curation, research, knowledge management, any domain where information overload is the real obstacle."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Introduce Randomness Into Your Learning Inputs"
  tagline="Structured learning deepens. Random inputs connect."
  explanation="Deliberate study in your field compounds existing knowledge. But the most surprising and generative insights often come from accidental collisions — a book outside your domain, a conversation with someone from a different field, a problem tackled with borrowed tools. Build in deliberate randomness: one unrelated read per month, conversations across industries, travel."
  relevance="Creative work, problem-solving, innovation, avoiding the blind spots that come from learning only within your own field."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Fast Is Slow"
  tagline="Rushing irreversible decisions costs far more than taking time to get them right."
  explanation="Speed is an asset in reversible, low-stakes situations — ship, test, iterate. But in high-stakes, hard-to-reverse decisions — hiring, partnerships, major commitments — rushing is expensive. The time saved in a bad hire is lost many times over in the damage and replacement cost. Slow down proportionally to the reversibility of the decision."
  relevance="Hiring, major investments, business partnerships, strategic pivots, any decision where the cost of being wrong is asymmetric to the cost of being slow."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="Focus Is Finite and Must Be Defended"
  tagline="Attention is a budget. Spend it or it gets spent for you."
  explanation="Deep focus is not a default state — it is the result of deliberate protection. Every open notification, every unnecessary meeting, every low-priority commitment is a withdrawal from a limited account. The people who produce the most and the best are not those with the most time, but those who most aggressively protect their attention."
  relevance="Productivity, creative work, deep work, leadership, any situation where the work that matters most requires sustained, uninterrupted thought."
>}}

{{< principle
  name="These Principles Apply Beyond Tech"
  tagline="Mental models don't care what industry you're in."
  explanation="Every model here was derived from one domain and applies to many. A writer faces the same challenges of quality, distribution, pricing power, and compounding reputation as a software founder. A musician building an audience navigates the same dynamics as a startup finding product-market fit. The latticework is industry-agnostic. The translation is the skill."
  relevance="Creative work, any domain outside the one where a model was first encountered, teaching and communicating ideas across disciplines."
>}}
