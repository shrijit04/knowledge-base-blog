> "Fish where the fish are." — Charlie Munger

Most investors waste years chasing hot tips or debating PE ratios, without asking the most basic question: **Is this even the right pond?**

Margins help answer that. They aren't just numbers — they reveal business quality, pricing power, and management discipline. A strong margin profile gives you insight into moats, industry structure, and capital efficiency without reading a 100-page annual report.

Learn to read margins properly and you'll instantly filter out 90% of market noise.

---

## The 8 layers

---

### Layer 1 — Compare margins across industries

*Before you study the fish, study the pond.*

One of the biggest mistakes retail investors make is jumping straight into stock picking without understanding the industry first. **Industry structure matters more than individual companies.**

Start by eliminating low-margin, capital-intensive, cutthroat sectors. If an entire industry runs on 8–9% margins — auto OEMs, airlines, sugar, paper, commodities — it signals no pricing power, low entry barriers, and brutal competition. Even the best company in a bad pond struggles to compound.

Instead, focus on industries where high margins are structurally possible: FMCG, specialty chemicals, CDMO/CRAMS, IT services, asset-light SaaS. These sectors often show 20–30% EBITDA margins, which signals pricing power, sticky customers, and capital efficiency.

Filtering by industry margin profile puts you in the high-quality zone from Day 1 and stacks the odds in your favour before you've even looked at a single company.

---

### Layer 2 — Find margin leaders within the industry

*Not all fish in a good pond are worth catching.*

Once you've chosen the right industry, find the "gorilla" — the companies that truly dominate and lead on margins.

- In IT services, TCS and Infosys maintain higher margins than smaller peers.
- In specialty chemicals, some firms post EBITDA margins above 25% while others sit at 10%.
- Divi's Labs dominates pharma API exports with a superior margin profile.
- Nestlé beats most FMCG players by 5–10 percentage points.

This difference tells you who really controls pricing and who is just surviving. Don't be fooled by industry tailwinds — the real winners convert those tailwinds into strong, sustainable margins.

Margin leaders have longevity in their growth, can reinvest at a high rate, and dominate over the long term.

---

### Layer 3 — Track margin trends over time

*Is the fish getting stronger or weaker?*

Once you've found the margin leader, track how margins are *moving*. Flat or improving margins usually mean the moat is intact and the business is scaling well. Shrinking margins are a red flag — either competition is rising, cost control is weakening, or pricing power is getting diluted.

**Asian Paints** — From FY14 to FY19, EBITDA margins expanded from 16% to 21%, driven by brand strength, wide distribution, and scale efficiencies. But after the entry of Birla Opus (Grasim) and JSW Paints, margins have dipped to ~18% in FY24. That dip signals the moat is being tested.

**Divi's Labs** — Gross margins have held in the 65–67% range for the last 10 years. Even through currency swings and global competition, no ground has been lost. That kind of stability tells you the business has a deep moat and high operational efficiency.

The best businesses don't just defend their margins — they quietly expand them over time.

---

### Layer 4 — Analyse the gap between gross and operating margin

*Is management keeping the fish healthy, or letting it waste energy?*

A company with high gross margins but weak operating margins is a red flag.

Gross margin shows the raw profitability of the product. Operating margin shows how efficiently the business is actually run. If overheads — employee costs, R&D, admin — are eating up profits, it signals poor cost control and inefficient operations.

**CAMS and CDSL** both manage this gap very well: tight spreads between gross and operating margins, demonstrating operational discipline.

**Many mid-cap FMCG players**, by contrast, burn cash on marketing and expansion without matching revenue growth — the gap tells the story before the earnings miss does.

This margin gap is a direct test of management quality and capital allocation skill.

---

### Layer 5 — Pair margins with ROCE

*Is the fish not just big, but also a strong swimmer?*

Margins alone don't tell the full story. **ROCE** (Return on Capital Employed) shows how well a company earns on the capital it invests. A business with 30% EBITDA margins but only 10% ROCE is either capital-inefficient or stuck in low-return projects.

Focus on companies that combine high margins with strong ROCE — ideally above 20–25% — along with smart reinvestment.

**Page Industries** consistently pairs strong margins with ROCE north of 40%, showing capital-efficient growth.

**Emami**, despite decent margins, has faced ROCE pressure due to aggressive marketing and expansion that hasn't generated enough profit relative to the capital deployed.

ROCE is your final check that profits are sustainable and scalable — not just one-offs.

---

### Layer 6 — Ask if the margins are defendable

*Is the fish swimming in clear water, or just riding a current?*

High margins look good, but are they real or artificially inflated? Margins can expand temporarily due to export arbitrage, currency moves, or commodity price swings — all of which can reverse quickly.

Focus on businesses where margins come from **durable moats**:
- Strong brands — Page Industries, Titan
- Intellectual property — Divi's Labs
- Network effects — CDSL

Many chemical companies saw margin spikes after 2021, but those gains disappeared by 2023–24, trapping retail investors. Meanwhile, Divi's Labs kept its margins steady because of structural moat and scale, not a fleeting tailwind.

**The question isn't how high the margins are. It's how long they'll stay there.** Fake margins are the fastest way to real losses.

---

### Layer 7 — Test margins through the cycle

*Can the fish still swim when the tide turns?*

It's easy to look good in calm waters. Only the strongest businesses hold their margins when headwinds hit — a slowdown, an inflation spike, a raw material shock.

> Great companies don't just grow margins during good times. They protect them when things get tough.

**Bajaj Finance** — Despite economic slowdowns and rising credit costs, it has maintained strong margins by managing risk and pricing power effectively.

**Jubilant FoodWorks** — Saw margin compression during inflationary periods, not because demand vanished, but because it couldn't pass on costs without hurting volumes. Same revenue story; very different margin story.

In markets, tides always turn. What matters is who can still swim upstream.

---

### Layer 8 — Match the margin profile to your investing style and horizon

*Is this fish the right catch for your fishing trip?*

Not every high-margin business fits every investor. Match the company's margin story to your risk tolerance and holding period.

- **Long-term investor?** Look for steadily improving margins, strong growth tailwinds, and disciplined capital allocation. These businesses reward patience and allow compounding over years.
- **Shorter horizon?** Cyclical margin recovery plays can work — but they require precise entry and exit. The margin framework still applies; you just weight the cycle layer (Layer 7) more heavily.

**Titan** started with modest margins but expanded them steadily over 15 years. **CDSL** quietly built pricing power in a niche market over a long runway.

Knowing your style and time horizon helps you avoid chasing the wrong fish and wasting effort on bad fits. Align your margin focus with your style — that's the key to lasting success.

## Quick-reference checklist

| Layer | Question to ask |
|---|---|
| 1. Industry | Does this pond structurally allow high margins? |
| 2. Leadership | Is this company the margin gorilla of its industry? |
| 3. Trend | Are margins expanding, stable, or compressing? |
| 4. Gross–Op gap | Is management controlling costs efficiently? |
| 5. ROCE | Do high margins translate to high returns on capital? |
| 6. Defensibility | Do margins come from a moat, or a temporary tailwind? |
| 7. Cycle | Do margins hold when conditions get tough? |
| 8. Fit | Does this margin profile match my style and horizon? |
