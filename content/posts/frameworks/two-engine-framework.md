> A stock's price isn't one number doing one thing. It's two engines multiplying each other.

## The core idea

Stock price ≈ **EPS × PE**.

That's not just an identity — it's a map of what actually drives returns. Every dollar of stock-price appreciation comes from one of two sources, and great investments come from getting **both** to move in your direction.

---

## Engine 1 — The EPS Engine

The EPS engine reflects the **fundamental growth** of the business. Net profit grows, and the stock price rises roughly in line with it.

- **Drivers:** revenue growth, operating leverage, share buybacks, margin expansion at the bottom line.
- **What it looks like in practice:** a company doubles earnings over five years. If nothing else changes, the stock roughly doubles.
- **Limit:** EPS growth is a linear engine. It rewards execution, but on its own it doesn't produce multi-baggers within a reasonable holding period.

---

## Engine 2 — The PE Engine

The PE engine reflects what **the market is willing to pay** for each dollar of earnings. It captures sentiment, perceived moat durability, and confidence in future margin expansion.

- **Drivers:** widening moat, accelerating growth, improving capital efficiency, sector re-ratings, narrative shifts.
- **What it looks like in practice:** a company's PE goes from 15× to 30× as the market realizes it has pricing power it didn't have before. Stock doubles — even before EPS moves.
- **Limit:** the PE engine is bounded. It can't expand forever, and when it contracts it gives back returns just as quickly as it gave them.

---

## Why this matters

Two investors can buy the **exact same company** and get wildly different outcomes:

| Investor | Entry PE | Entry EPS | Held for | EPS growth | PE at exit | Return |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | 15× | $1 | 10 years | 4× | 30× | **8×** |
| B | 50× | $1 | 10 years | 4× | 15× | **1.2×** |

Same earnings trajectory. Same business. The difference is entirely where the **PE engine** was at entry. Investor B paid for the future already; investor A got the future and the re-rating.

---

## How I use it

1. **Decompose the historical return.** When studying a past multi-bagger, ask: how much came from EPS, how much from PE? You'll usually find both moved together.
2. **Check the PE engine at entry.** If multiple expansion has already played out, you're betting on EPS alone — that's a fine bet, but size it accordingly.
3. **Look for moat inflections.** The PE engine re-rates hardest when the market belatedly recognizes that a business is more durable than it thought. Those moments are where asymmetric returns live.
4. **Avoid "finished" stories.** A great business at a fully-priced multiple is a fundamentally different investment than the same business at a fair multiple — even if the underlying earnings power is identical.
