The Compounding Ladder

A first-principles test for what to own and what to pay — five gates, one causal chain, and the price math that ties it together.
May 31, 2026 · map[email:shrijit@umich.edu name:Shrijit Patel]

Capital creates value only when it earns more than it costs — for as long as that lasts, and only if the cash actually reaches you. Price decides how much of that compounding you keep.

Everything below is this sentence, expanded. If you remember nothing else, remember this.

The five gates#

Run them top to bottom. They are not a checklist of separate virtues — they are one causal chain. A broken rung voids every rung below it.

The hub of all five is ROIC (return on invested capital). Each gate asks something about ROIC — is it real, is it high enough, will it last, does it reach me, and what am I paying for it.

Gate 1 — Real? (Governance)#

Are the reported numbers true?

Cash flow tracks earnings. No promoter pledging. Clean related-party dealings and revenue recognition.

If the numbers are fiction, gates 2–5 are analysing fiction. This is why it runs first.

Gate 2 — Engine? (ROIC vs. cost of capital)#

Does each dollar reinvested earn well above what that dollar costs?

Value compounds at roughly reinvestment rate × ROIC, not at the headline EPS growth rate. Growth above the cost of capital builds value. Growth below the cost of capital destroys value while the headline number rises — a common trap in capital-intensive sectors.

Gate 3 — Moat? (Durability)#

What stops competition from dragging ROIC back down to the cost of capital?

Switching costs, scale economies, network effects, brand, regulation — any source of structural advantage that makes it costly or impossible for competitors to replicate the engine.

The moat’s only job is to answer “how many years does the engine run?” — and that number is exactly what the multiple is charging you for.

Gate 4 — Conduit? (Capital allocation)#

Does the cash the engine throws off actually reach shareholders?

Reinvested at high returns, used to buy back stock when cheap, or paid out — instead of wasted on empire-building or vanity acquisitions. This is the link between business returns and per-share returns. A great engine with a poor allocator compounds your money badly.

Gate 5 — Price? (The multiple)#

Are you prepaying for more years of flawless gates 1–4 than you can actually defend?

A great business at a perfect price is where most permanent losses originate. See the price math below.

The 60-second pass#

Real? → Engine? → Moat? → Conduit? → Price?

Four solid yeses on the business and a defensible price is the whole game.

Memory hook: Real Engines Move Cash Profitably.

The price math#

At zero growth, the payback period is the PE. 3× = 3-year payback. 30× = 30 years. 100× = 100. Growth compresses this, but non-linearly — and the compression requires the growth to actually materialise.

The multiple is prepaid compounding years. A high PE is a claim that gates 1–4 hold flawlessly for a long time. You are buying time, and betting it shows up.

Your return decomposes into two factors:

One factor you can analyse (growth). One you are largely guessing (the exit multiple).

The risk lives in the exit multiple, not the growth. From a high entry multiple, the re-rating is a near-certain drag — you can be exactly right about the business and still lose because the market re-prices the pie. (Over very long holds — 25–30 years — this washes out and your return converges to the business’s own compounding.)

The present-value ceiling: 1 / (hurdle rate − growth rate). Above this multiple, no amount of time recovers the price in present-value terms. That is “too expensive” — defined precisely, not as a feeling.

Kill-switches — these override everything above#

Two conditions can make an otherwise qualifying business un-ownable:

A fragile balance sheet meeting cyclicality. Leverage turns a temporary downturn into a permanent loss. The business that survives a cycle intact inherits the market share of those that don’t.

A multiple that already prices perfection. No margin for the inevitable disappointment. The only surprises left are bad ones — and they arrive.

A business can pass all five gates and still fail either of these.

The whole framework in one breath#

Own businesses that reinvest above their cost of capital (Engine), protected long enough to matter (Moat), with the cash reaching you (Conduit), on numbers you can trust (Real) — and pay a price that doesn’t already assume it all goes perfectly (Price).

Growth without ROIC is motion without progress. A great business bought at any price is just a slower way to overpay.

Pairs well with#

  • The Two Engine Framework — the EPS and PE engines map directly onto the Engine/Moat gates and the Price gate respectively.
  • The Margin Framework — margin analysis is the most practical diagnostic tool for gates 2 and 3: does the engine run, and is it structurally protected?
