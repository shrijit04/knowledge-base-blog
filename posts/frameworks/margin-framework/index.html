The Margin Framework

Profitability lives in layers — Layer 1 scales, Layer 2 charges, Layer 3 compounds. The deeper a business reaches, the longer its runway.
May 25, 2026 · map[email:shrijit@umich.edu name:Shrijit Patel]

Margin isn’t a number. It’s a stack — and each layer tells you something different about how the business compounds.

The core idea#

Two businesses can report identical operating margins and still be wildly different investments. The Margin Framework breaks profitability into three layers, each of which tells you where the profit is coming from and how durable it is.

The deeper a business reaches into the stack, the more its margins behave like a compounding asset rather than a cyclical one.

Layer 1 — Core operating margins#

The base layer: gross margin and operating margin. These reflect the cost structure of producing and selling the product.

  • What to look for: operating leverage — profits growing faster than revenue as fixed costs get absorbed across a bigger base.
  • What it tells you: is the unit economics fundamentally sound? Can the business afford to grow?
  • What it doesn’t tell you: anything about pricing power or durability. A high Layer 1 margin from a commodity boom evaporates as fast as it arrived.

Layer 1 is necessary but not sufficient. Every great business has it; lots of mediocre businesses also have it temporarily.

Layer 2 — Pricing power and value-added services#

The middle layer: the ability to charge premium prices, or layer high-margin add-ons on top of a core product.

  • What to look for: brand strength, switching costs, ecosystem lock-in, mission-critical positioning, accessory/service attach rates.
  • Examples: Apple selling AppleCare, accessories, and Services on top of the device; Mastercard charging incremental basis points for value-added analytics; ASML charging service revenue on installed base.
  • Why it matters: Layer 2 margin doesn’t move with input costs. It moves with how indispensable the business is to its customer. That makes it far more durable than Layer 1.

Layer 3 — Asset-light scalability#

The top layer: businesses with “floating DNA.” They can expand revenue — often globally — without proportional capex, headcount, debt, or share dilution.

  • What to look for: software and platforms, brands with licensing models, marketplaces, IP-rich businesses, anything where the marginal cost of the next dollar of revenue is near zero.
  • Why it matters: Layer 3 is where compounding stops being incremental and starts being exponential. Revenue can grow for decades without the business consuming the capital it earns. That capital then flows back to shareholders or into new high-return investments.

If Layer 1 is “the business works,” and Layer 2 is “the business has power,” Layer 3 is “the business has runway.”

How I use it#

  1. Diagnose the layer composition. When a company posts strong margins, ask: which layer is doing the work? A 25% operating margin from Layer 1 alone is a different beast from a 25% margin where Layer 2 and 3 are most of the story.
  2. Stress-test durability. Layer 1 margins mean-revert. Layer 2 margins erode only when the moat erodes. Layer 3 margins generally expand over time. Underwrite accordingly.
  3. Look for layer transitions. The biggest re-ratings come when a business visibly graduates from Layer 1 → Layer 2, or Layer 2 → Layer 3. These are inflection points worth watching.
  4. Avoid “Layer 1 trap” investments. A cyclical business at peak Layer 1 margins, valued as if those margins are permanent, is one of the most reliable ways to lose money.

Pairs well with#

  • The Two Engine Framework — margin expansion is one of the cleanest catalysts for PE re-rating. As a business climbs the Margin stack, the market awards a higher multiple, which compounds on top of the underlying earnings growth. The two frameworks aren’t separate — they’re the same compounding machine viewed from two angles.
