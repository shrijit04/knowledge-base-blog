Principles I Keep Coming Back To

A living collection of mental models and principles worth remembering.
April 25, 2026 · map[email:shrijit@umich.edu name:Shrijit Patel]

A running list of ideas I find myself returning to — collected here so I can grow it over time.

Asymmetric Loss Not knowing is better than knowing wrong.
▼

When the downside of false confidence outweighs the downside of uncertainty, staying ignorant is the safer position. A wrong map actively misleads you; no map at least keeps you alert. The danger isn't the gap in your knowledge — it's the illusion that the gap doesn't exist.

Relevant when: Risk assessment, investing, medical diagnosis, any high-stakes decision where acting on bad information is worse than pausing.

Critical Mass Nothing happens — until suddenly everything happens.
▼

Most systems have a threshold below which effort produces negligible results and above which momentum becomes self-sustaining. The growth curve looks flat, then vertical. The hard work isn't scaling — it's surviving long enough to reach the threshold.

Relevant when: Building habits, growing audiences, startup traction, network effects, social movements, any domain where compounding only kicks in after a certain density.

Inversion Figure out what you want to avoid, then don't do that.
▼

Instead of asking 'how do I succeed?', ask 'what would guarantee failure?' Mapping the failure modes is often clearer and more actionable than imagining success. Avoiding the obvious pitfalls eliminates most of the variance; success tends to follow.

Relevant when: Problem-solving, strategy, risk management, habit design, any situation where the path forward is unclear but the paths to failure are visible.
